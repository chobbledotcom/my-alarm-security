# SEO & Content Quality Report (Private)

**Internal document — `noindex`, excluded from the sitemap and from site collections. Not linked from any public navigation.**

This is a review of the site against Google's helpful-content quality guidelines (content quality, expertise/E-E-A-T, presentation, and the comparative questions). Links below go to the live pages so you can see each point in context.

### 1. Thin, near-template location pages (highest priority)

This is the biggest gap against the guidelines. The strong location pages clearly come from someone who works the area — compare the model pages with the templated ones:

**Model standard:** [Bromley](/pages/bromley/) and [Orpington](/pages/orpington/) — unique local geography, a real attributed review quote, links to nearby job write-ups, genuine local detail (the Orpington food-truck aside, the ULEZ section).

**Thin / near-duplicate:** [Hayes](/pages/hayes/), [Keston](/pages/keston/), [Meopham](/pages/meopham/) and [New Ash Green](/pages/new-ash-green/) share a near-identical opening sentence with only the town name swapped; [Petts Wood](/pages/petts-wood/) is ~300 words of interchangeable boilerplate. Google's guidelines target exactly this pattern — "scaled content" and "doorway pages" — and the comparative question _"does this exist solely by someone guessing what might rank?"_ is one a reviewer would answer badly here.

**Fix:** bring the thin pages up to the Bromley/Orpington standard. One genuinely local thing per page is enough — a real job you did there, a road or landmark, a known local issue — plus a real review from that area and a link to a relevant blog post. The raw material already exists in the blog; it just needs distributing onto the area pages.

_Suggested approach: upgrade 3–4 pages first, confirm the tone, then roll the pattern across the rest._

### 2. Unsourced statistics on the homepage

The [homepage](/) states homes with no security are "five times more likely to be broken in to" and that "over 70% of people only have a... system installed after... a break in." These are presented as fact with no source, which works against the guidelines' sourcing question. Either cite them (ONS, police, or a recognised security-industry body) or reframe them as clearly attributed.

### 3. No author attribution on blog posts

The blog is the site's best asset — first-hand, expert write-ups like the [Orpington service job](/blog/alarm-system-service-maintenance-in-orpington/) and the [Eltham alarm and CCTV upgrade](/blog/burglar-alarm-cctv-upgrade-in-eltham-se9/). But they carry no byline. Adding "by Matt, MyAlarm Security" with a link to the [About page](/pages/about-us/) converts that hidden expertise into a visible trust signal — directly answering the E-E-A-T question about evidence of the author behind the content.

### 4. Reconcile the headline experience figure (owner decision)

The wording is now consistent at "over 30 years", but there's an underlying factual question to confirm: the [FAQ](/faq/) states Matt has worked in the industry "since 1998", which is 28 years as of 2026 — slightly under the "over 30 years" used elsewhere. Worth deciding which is correct (e.g. experience predating the 1998 formal start, or adjust the headline figure) so the two never contradict each other.

## What's already working well

Recorded so it isn't lost in future edits:

- **The blog** is original, first-hand reporting — the strongest content on the site and exactly what the helpful-content system rewards.
- **Category and product pages** ([Burglar Alarms](/categories/burglar-alarms/), [Standard System](/products/standard-system/)) are comprehensive and clearly written by a practitioner.
- **The [FAQ](/faq/)** is specific and honestly scoped (no police monitoring; DIY systems not serviced) — a page worth bookmarking.
- **Trust signals**: named founder, DBS checked, public liability, 100+ reviews, specific brand knowledge (Pyronix, Hikvision ColorVu, Texecom, Galaxy).
- No ad clutter; mobile-friendly build.

The ceiling here is high — the work is mostly about lifting the weakest pages up to the standard the best ones already set.
