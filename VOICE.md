# Voice & Tone

This site has a distinct voice: dry, plain-spoken, sceptical of marketing-speak, recognisably a small Sidcup alarm and CCTV business without doing fake London character. It is how a husband-and-wife security company might write about its own work - not how a copywriter would write it for them.

Matt is the person most customers meet on the tools. Rachel is part of the business too, especially around calls, bookings, admin and follow-up. The copy should feel like it comes from that sort of setup: two people running a careful local business, not a regional security brand pretending to be personal.

This is a guide, not a template. Slavish application is what makes copy sound generated.

## The Trap To Avoid

The source corpus below is people **talking**, spontaneously. Their rhetorical patterns - punchy fragments, lists of three with a comic third, deflating one-liners - work because they are spoken, not written. Lift those patterns straight into website copy and you have turned spontaneous wit into ad copy. You can hear the agency.

The localness in this site should come from **content** (Sidcup, Bexley, Eltham, Orpington, Chislehurst, actual systems, actual jobs) and **word choice** (a call-out, the panel, the siren, shoe covers, a fair bit, on the tools) - not from sentence structure or mockney business.

### The WhatsApp Test

Read the line out loud. Could Matt type it on his phone between jobs, or could Rachel send it back to a customer after checking the diary? If it has a copywriter's structure - a punchy fragment, a parallel-list closer, a one-line summary that "lands" - no, they probably would not. Take the polish off.

## Source Corpus

Don't lean on any single voice - that becomes a tribute act. The shared DNA across all of these is what we are after:

- **Small trade business writing** - especially owner-written updates where the detail is better than the polish.
- **Checkatrade and Google review language** - customers saying what actually mattered: turning up, tidy work, clear explanations, no pressure, fixing the thing properly.
- **Fred Dibnah** - for craft, materials, and proper specificity, not for accent.
- **John Prescott** - plain-speaking working-class register; sceptical of polish.
- **Sean Dyche** - practical, dry, slightly impatient with nonsense.
- **Caroline Aherne** - ordinary domestic conversation, but do not import punchlines.
- **Coronation Street** - for plain working dialogue, especially the ability to say a lot without dressing it up.
- **Good local Facebook or Nextdoor replies** - the useful ones where someone explains what they can do, what it costs, and when they can get there.

When stuck, don't generate - read a few reviews or job posts from the site. But remember they are mostly customers talking. Don't turn their offhand comments into slogans.

## Principles

### 1. First Person Where It Makes Sense

"We run MyAlarm Security from Sidcup" is more honest than "We provide professional security solutions". Switching between "I" and "we" is natural when the page needs it. Matt can talk about the installation. Rachel can talk about booking, prices and what photos to send over. The business as a whole can say "we".

Small businesses talk like that. Corporate voices don't.

### 2. Specifics Over Abstractions

Sidcup bungalow, not "residential property". Pyronix, Texecom, Hikvision and Scantronic, not "market-leading equipment". Door contacts, PIRs, bell boxes, batteries, panels, keyfobs, apps and camera recorders, not "integrated protection".

The voice gets its credibility from being specific.

Bad:

> We deliver tailored security solutions for domestic and commercial clients across the region.

Better:

> We fit burglar alarms and CCTV around Sidcup, Bexley, Eltham and Orpington, and most jobs start with a quick look at the property, the old panel if there is one, and where the cameras or sensors actually need to go.

### 3. Plain Word Choice

"A few quid" not "significant savings". "Hold up well" not "perform reliably". "On the tools" not "field operations". "Call-out" not "attendance event". Use trade vocabulary where it helps the customer understand the job.

If you wouldn't say it on the phone to a customer, don't write it.

### 4. Looseness Over Polish

Long sentences with sub-clauses are fine. Hedges are fine. "Usually", "around", "it depends on the system", "send us a photo of the panel" and "we can normally tell from that" all sound like a real business because that is how the work actually is.

The opposite - short, polished sentences with parallel structure - is what marketing copy sounds like.

### 5. Complete Sentences, No Punchline Closers

The voice never uses sentence fragments. Every sentence has a subject and a verb. Fragments masquerading as sentences - "No hard sell, no unnecessary jobs." or "The lot." or "No messing." or "Same day repairs." - don't belong in the house voice, even when they sound emphatic or efficient. They are a copywriter's tic.

This applies anywhere in the copy, not just at paragraph ends. Rewrite fragments by absorbing them into the surrounding sentence, or by giving them their own subject and verb:

- Bad: "We only recommend work that actually needs doing. No hard sell, no unnecessary jobs."
- Good: "We only recommend work that actually needs doing, and we don't push hard sells or tack on jobs you don't need."

Even grammatically complete sentences shouldn't be used as cinematic punchline closers - things like "MyAlarm Security followed." or "And the rest is history." A complete sentence is necessary but not sufficient; the structure also has to read like prose, not like a tagline.

Specific patterns to avoid:

- Single-word or fragment closers: "The lot." / "That's the whole arrangement." / "No messing."
- Multi-fragment summaries: "£95 call-out. Same-day repairs. No hard sell."
- Cinematic one-line summaries: "MyAlarm Security followed." / "And the rest is history."
- Lists of three where the third item is comic or undercutting.
- The X / X / X - Y structure: "Not a call centre, not a franchise, not a regional manager - Matt and Rachel."
- The "and yes, it works round here" handle-the-objection move.

Bullets and headings are exempt - they are not sentences.

### 6. Trust The Reader

Don't explain the joke. Don't justify the claim. Don't repeat yourself. The voice is economical because it assumes intelligence on the other end.

It is also careful because alarms and CCTV affect people's homes, privacy and money. Dry does not mean careless.

## Phrase Pool

Words and turns of phrase that fit the voice when they appear naturally - never as 1:1 substitution:

- "a few quid"
- "a fair bit"
- "hold up well"
- "on the tools"
- "round here"
- "we've worked with for years"
- "send us a photo of the panel"
- "we can usually tell from that"
- "it depends what system you've got"
- "tidy up as we go"
- "the old bell box"
- "the app side of it"
- "a proper service"

Build this up over time as more copy gets written. One or two per page is plenty.

## No-Go List

Burnt out by local trade adverts, national security brands and fake London copy. Avoid:

- "no nonsense" as a badge of character
- "honest as the day is long" / "salt of the earth"
- "your trusted local experts"
- "peace of mind" unless it is genuinely the customer's phrase
- "protect what matters most"
- "security solutions"
- "bespoke packages" when "we can add extra sensors or cameras" is clearer
- "family values"
- "small enough to care, big enough to cope"
- "East End", "geezer", "matey" or any mockney styling
- phonetic accent spelling
- "proper" as an empty intensifier
- "no job too small"

These can appear inside quotation marks if a customer says them. They don't belong in house voice.

## Process

1. Write the page in plain English first. Don't reach for the voice yet.
2. Read it aloud. Mark every sentence that sounds like it could be on a brochure.
3. Apply the WhatsApp test to each marked sentence: could Matt or Rachel send this to a customer? If not, take the polish off - usually by adding the real detail, adding a hedge, or breaking up a too-neat structure.
4. Read aloud again. If you smirk, pull back further.

## Where To Use It

- About page
- Homepage section intros where SEO allows it
- News and job write-ups
- Product explanations where plainness helps
- Transitions and link text

## Where NOT To Use It

- Legal text and policies
- Technical specs and accreditation pages
- Contact details and form labels
- Prices, exclusions and call-out terms where ambiguity costs the reader money or clarity
- CCTV privacy wording, recording notices or anything that needs careful compliance language

Inconsistency between pages is fine. Different pages have different jobs.

## Anti-Patterns

The specific failure modes of trying too hard:

- **Fragment "sentences"** - "The lot." / "No hard sell." / "Same day call-outs." Anywhere in copy, not just at paragraph end. See principle 5.
- **Cinematic one-liners** - "MyAlarm Security followed." / "And the rest is history."
- **The deflating undercut** - used once it is a flourish; used as a pattern it is marketing.
- **The rule-of-three list** - "Safer, smarter, simpler" sounds like a security brochure. Don't do it.
- **The handle-the-objection move** - "and yes, CCTV is worth it round here" / "and yes, we really are local". Pure copywriter device.
- **Punchline-delivery dashes** - "Not X, not Y, not Z - Matt." A hyphen used as an ordinary explanatory aside is fine. House style: use single hyphens with spaces around them. Never use double hyphens or em-dashes - both have been normalised out of the codebase.
- **Tag questions on every paragraph** - even one per page is often too many. Use only when it genuinely lets the reader in.
- **Trying to be funny** - the voice is dry, not jokey. If a line gets a laugh, fine. If it is *for* the laugh, cut it.
- **Generic local markers as a substitute for voice** - anyone can mention Sidcup and Bexley. The voice has to live in *content* and *structure*, not just place names.
